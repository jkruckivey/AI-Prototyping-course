The Everyday Problem

Think of it like this: You're looking at job postings and wondering "How much should I expect to earn based on my experience?" This is exactly what linear regression helps us figure out!

A Canadian MBA program collected real data from 15 graduates. Notice the pattern - more experience generally means higher salary:

üë∂ 2 years ‚Üí $56k CAD üöÄ 5 years ‚Üí $77k CAD üí™ 8 years ‚Üí $100k CAD üèÜ 12 years ‚Üí $140k CAD

2

Drawing a Line Through the Dots

Imagine this: You have dots scattered on a graph and you want to draw the "best" straight line through them. That line becomes your salary predictor!

Every straight line needs just 2 numbers to describe it completely:

üìà Slope: How steep is the line?
Example: "$7,300 CAD more salary per year of experience"
üìç Y-intercept: Where does the line cross the Y-axis?
Example: "$37,000 CAD starting salary with 0 years experience"

The magic: Once we find these 2 numbers, we can predict ANY salary!

3

Finding the Perfect Line

Here's the challenge: There are infinite ways to draw a line through dots. How do we find the "best" one?

Think of it like archery: We measure how far each prediction "misses" the actual salary. The line with the smallest total "miss" wins!

üéØ Good Line: Predictions close to actual salaries
Average miss: Only $200 CAD off per person
‚ùå Bad Line: Predictions far from actual salaries
Average miss: $1,600 CAD off per person

MSE (Mean Squared Error) is our "accuracy score" - lower is better!

üìö Key Concepts

Parameters

Œ≤ (slope) and Œµ (intercept) are learned by the model during training. The algorithm adjusts these values to minimize prediction errors.

Loss Function (MSE)

Mean Squared Error measures how far predictions are from actual values. We square errors so positive and negative errors don't cancel out.

Optimization

The training process iteratively adjusts parameters to minimize the loss function, finding the best-fitting line through the data.

Dimensionality

1D: One feature (line)
2D: Two features (plane)
N-D: Multiple features (hyperplane)
