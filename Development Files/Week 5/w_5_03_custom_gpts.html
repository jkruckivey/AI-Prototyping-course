 Custom GPTs & AI Assistants

What this page covers

How to create custom GPT assistants tailored to specific tasks and audiences. You'll learn to configure personas, add knowledge bases (RAG), enable capabilities, and build specialized AI tools without coding—from teaching assistants to business chatbots.

 Why Create Custom GPTs?

Limitations of standard ChatGPT

Standard GPT-4 or other foundation models have inherent limitations:

  • Training cutoff: Knowledge ends at a specific date (e.g., end of 2023)
  • Generic responses: Not fine-tuned for your specific domain or audience
  • No specialized behavior: Lacks guardrails, tone, or workflow you need
  • No private knowledge: Can't access your documents, policies, or proprietary information
  • Limited actions: Can't integrate with your specific tools or systems

Benefits of custom GPTs

Custom GPTs solve these problems by enabling:

  • Persona customization: Define exact tone, expertise level, and communication style
  • Knowledge integration: Upload PDFs, documents, policies—instant RAG system
  • Guardrails: Specify what the AI should and shouldn't do
  • Workflow design: Create step-by-step processes the AI follows automatically
  • Capabilities: Enable web search, code interpreter, image generation, custom actions
  • Shareability: Publish for your team or publicly in the GPT store

 Creating a Custom GPT

Access and setup

Requirements:

  • ChatGPT Plus subscription (or similar for other platforms)
  • Access to GPT builder interface
  • Documents/knowledge to upload (optional but recommended)

Getting started:

  1. Click "Explore" → "Create a GPT" in ChatGPT
  2. Choose between guided builder or manual configuration
  3. Start with basic settings: name, description, instructions

Name and description

Name: Choose a clear, descriptive name

  • Example: "Math TAI" (Teaching Assistant with AI)
  • Example: "HR Policy Assistant"
  • Example: "Marketing Content Generator"

Description: Short summary (appears in GPT library)

  • 1-2 sentences explaining what the GPT does
  • Who it's designed for
  • Key capabilities

Instructions (the persona prompt)

This is your system prompt—the foundation of your custom GPT's behavior.

Math TAI example (abbreviated):

You are a friendly, approachable university-level mathematics professor who explains concepts in a conversational and empathetic tone. You help first-year psychology students, many of whom have limited backgrounds in mathematics, to understand course material effectively.

When a student asks about course content:
1. Explain concepts clearly and informally, like a relaxed but thorough chat
2. Look up relevant exercises from past exams in your knowledge base
3. Always ask if they want to go through the solution step-by-step
4. After explanation, run "search transcript" to find related lecture video timestamps
5. Share clickable video links with timestamps if found

When a student asks about course structure:
- Answer only based on the course outline in your knowledge base
- Include direct quotations to support your answer

When providing numerical solutions:
- Automatically use Python to verify accuracy before finalizing

At the end of every conversation:
- Ask "Is there anything else I can help with?"
- List available commands: Search transcript, Email help, Grade scheme

Key elements: Persona, audience, behavior rules, workflow steps, guardrails, helpful reminders

Conversation starters

Provide example prompts to guide users:

  • "I don't understand circular permutations. Can you explain?"
  • "I missed an online quiz. What should I do?"
  • "Show me a past exam question about probability trees."
  • "What's the grading scheme for this course?"

These appear as clickable buttons when users start a conversation with your GPT.

 Knowledge Base (RAG Integration)

What is the knowledge base?

The Knowledge section implements RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) automatically:

  1. You upload documents (PDFs, text files, spreadsheets)
  2. OpenAI creates a vector database from your documents
  3. When users ask questions, the orchestrator searches this database
  4. Relevant chunks are retrieved and added to the prompt
  5. The LLM responds using both its training and your documents

You don't see it happening, but every query triggers retrieval → augmentation → generation.

What to upload

Math TAI example knowledge base:

  • Course content map: Week-by-week topic guide
  • Past final exams: Solutions to 5+ different exams (PDFs)
  • Exercise solutions: PDFs with worked examples
  • Course outline: Grading scheme, deadlines, policies

Business use cases:

  • Product manuals and troubleshooting guides
  • Company policies and HR documents
  • Legal templates and contract examples
  • Brand guidelines and content examples
  • Historical data, reports, and analyses

Citing sources

Instruct your GPT to cite knowledge base sources:

"When answering questions about course structure, include direct quotations from the outline in your knowledge base to support your answer."

Example response:

"Since only the best five out of six quizzes count towards your final grade, you don't need to do anything for your first missed quiz. According to the course outline: 'Students do not need to request academic consideration for the first missed quiz.'"

 Capabilities and Actions

Standard capabilities (built-in)

Enable these with a single click:

  • Web search: Access current information beyond training cutoff
  • Code interpreter: Execute Python for calculations, data analysis, visualizations
  • Canvas: Generate and edit documents in a side panel
  • Image generation (DALL-E): Create images, diagrams, visualizations

Math TAI enabled capabilities:

  • ✅ Web search (for current information if needed)
  • ✅ Canvas (for generating educational materials)
  • ✅ Image generation (for probability trees, diagrams)
  • ✅ Code interpreter (for verifying numerical answers)

Custom actions (advanced)

Create custom integrations with external systems:

  • REST APIs: Connect to your own web services
  • Databases: Query and update data stores
  • Email: Send drafts, notifications, or automated messages
  • Zapier: Integrate with thousands of apps
  • Custom endpoints: Any service with an API

Math TAI custom action example:

  • Search Transcript: Queries a custom website with course video transcripts
  • Returns: Matching timestamps and video links
  • User sees: "Here's the lecture where we discussed this [video link with timestamp]"

Note: Custom actions require JSON schema and API knowledge, but ChatGPT can help you create them.

Action workflow example

Student: "I'm not getting circular permutations. Can you explain?"

Math TAI:
1. Explains concept in friendly tone (persona)
2. Retrieves past exam question from knowledge base (RAG)
3. Walks through solution step-by-step
4. Triggers "search transcript" custom action
5. Receives video timestamps from external API
6. Shares clickable link: "Here's the lecture at 12:35 where we covered this"
7. Uses code interpreter to verify numerical example
8. Asks: "Would you like to try a similar problem?"

All of this happens automatically based on your instructions and enabled capabilities.

 Guardrails and Safety

Critical: Prevent Hallucinations and Errors

Always include explicit guardrails in your instructions:

  • Scope limits: "Only answer questions about mathematics. Politely decline other topics."
  • Action limits: "You cannot send emails directly. Draft them for user review only."
  • Citation requirements: "Always cite your source when using knowledge base documents."
  • Uncertainty handling: "If you're not sure, say 'I don't have enough information' instead of guessing."
  • Sensitive data: "Never share student grades or personal information."

Hallucination example: Math TAI said "email sent" when it couldn't actually send emails—the guardrail "You don't send emails" would have prevented this false claim.

 Publishing and Sharing

Sharing options

  • Only me: Private, for personal use
  • Anyone with a link: Share URL with specific people/teams
  • Public (GPT Store): Listed in OpenAI's GPT directory for everyone

Publishing tips

  1. Test thoroughly: Use your GPT extensively before sharing
  2. Gather feedback: Share with a small group first, iterate based on feedback
  3. Monitor usage: Check analytics to see how people use your GPT
  4. Update regularly: Add new knowledge, refine instructions, fix issues
  5. Clear documentation: Provide a description and conversation starters

Helpful Tip

Start simple, then expand. Begin with clear instructions and a small knowledge base. Test with real users. Add capabilities and actions incrementally. Even basic custom GPTs deliver immense value when properly scoped.
