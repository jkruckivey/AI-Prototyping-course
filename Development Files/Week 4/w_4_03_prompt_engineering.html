 Prompt Engineering

What this page covers

Prompt engineering is the art and science of crafting effective instructions for LLMs. You'll learn how to get better results through zero-shot, one-shot, and few-shot learning, apply proven prompt patterns, and use advanced techniques like chain of thought reasoning.

 What is Prompt Engineering?

Core definition

Prompt engineering is the practice of designing inputs (prompts) that guide LLMs to produce desired outputs. It's about communicating your intent clearly and structuring requests to leverage the model's capabilities.

Why it matters:

  • LLMs are powerful but need clear guidance
  • Small changes in prompts can dramatically affect output quality
  • Good prompting is often cheaper and faster than fine-tuning
  • Prompt engineering is accessible—no ML expertise required

Components of a good prompt

Effective prompts typically include several components:

  • Role/Persona: Who should the model act as? (e.g., "You are an expert financial analyst")
  • Context: Background information needed to complete the task
  • Task: Clear description of what you want the model to do
  • Format: How should the output be structured? (bullet points, JSON, table, etc.)
  • Examples: Sample inputs and desired outputs (for few-shot learning)
  • Constraints: Limitations or requirements (length, tone, style)

Before & After examples

How prompt engineering improves results
Scenario Weak Prompt Strong Prompt
Email summarization "Summarize this email" "You are an executive assistant. Summarize this email in 3 bullet points focusing on: (1) action items, (2) deadlines, (3) key decisions needed."

 Zero-shot, One-shot, Few-shot Learning

LLMs can learn tasks from examples provided in the prompt itself—no retraining required. The number of examples determines the approach.

Zero-shot learning

Zero-shot: Give instructions without examples. Rely on the model's pre-trained knowledge.

When to use:

  • Common tasks the model already knows (summarization, translation, Q&A)
  • When you want quick results without crafting examples
  • Tasks with clear, standard definitions

Example: Zero-shot Sentiment Analysis

Prompt:

Classify the sentiment of this customer review as Positive, Negative, or Neutral: "The product arrived on time but the quality was disappointing." Sentiment:

Model output: "Negative"

One-shot learning

One-shot: Provide one example of the task before the actual input.

When to use:

  • Tasks with specific formatting requirements
  • When zero-shot results are inconsistent
  • To demonstrate tone, style, or structure

Example: One-shot Classification

Prompt:

Classify support tickets by urgency level. Example: Ticket: "System is down, can't process orders" Urgency: CRITICAL Ticket: "How do I reset my password?" Urgency:

Model output: "LOW"

Few-shot learning

Few-shot: Provide multiple examples (typically 3-10) to establish a clear pattern.

When to use:

  • Complex or specialized tasks
  • When you need consistent, precise outputs
  • Domain-specific tasks the model hasn't seen much of in training
  • Tasks requiring specific formatting or structure

Example: Few-shot Entity Extraction

Prompt:

Extract company name, role, and action from job postings. Example 1: Text: "Microsoft is hiring a Senior Engineer in cloud computing" Company: Microsoft | Role: Senior Engineer | Domain: Cloud Computing Example 2: Text: "Amazon seeks Data Scientist for recommendation systems" Company: Amazon | Role: Data Scientist | Domain: Recommendation Systems Example 3: Text: "Google looking for ML Researcher in NLP" Company: Google | Role: ML Researcher | Domain: NLP Text: "Meta needs AI Product Manager for generative AI products" Company:

Model output: "Meta | Role: AI Product Manager | Domain: Generative AI"

Choosing the right approach

When to use each approach
Approach Pros Cons Best For
Zero-shot Fast, simple, no examples needed Less control, may be inconsistent Common tasks, exploratory work
One-shot Quick improvement, minimal context May not capture full pattern Format demonstration, tone setting
Few-shot High accuracy, consistent format Uses more tokens (costs more), requires good examples Complex tasks, production systems

 Proven Prompt Patterns

Research and practice have identified effective prompt patterns that consistently improve LLM outputs. These patterns are reusable templates you can adapt to your needs.

Persona pattern

Pattern: Tell the model to act as a specific role or expert.

Template: "You are [ROLE]. [Task instructions]"

Why it works: Activates relevant knowledge and appropriate tone/style from training data.

Examples

  • "You are a senior financial analyst. Review this investment proposal..."
  • "You are a technical writer specializing in API documentation. Write..."
  • "You are a compassionate customer service representative. Respond to..."
  • "You are a Python expert focusing on code optimization. Refactor..."

Audience pattern

Pattern: Specify the intended audience for the output.

Template: "Explain [TOPIC] for [AUDIENCE]"

Why it works: Adjusts complexity, terminology, and examples to audience level.

Examples

  • "Explain machine learning for a CEO with no technical background"
  • "Describe this API endpoint for junior developers"
  • "Summarize this legal contract for a high school student"
  • "Explain quantum computing for experienced software engineers"

Structure/Format pattern

Pattern: Explicitly define the output structure or format.

Template: "Provide your response in the following format: [STRUCTURE]"

Why it works: Ensures consistent, parseable outputs—critical for production systems.

Examples

  • "Return results as JSON with keys: name, category, confidence_score"
  • "Structure your analysis with: 1) Executive Summary, 2) Key Findings, 3) Recommendations"
  • "Format as a markdown table with columns: Feature, Pros, Cons, Cost"
  • "Respond in exactly 3 bullet points, each under 20 words"

Cognitive verifier pattern

Pattern: Ask the model to break down complex questions into sub-questions, answer each, then synthesize.

Template: "When answering [QUESTION], generate sub-questions that help answer it. Answer each sub-question. Then provide the final answer."

Why it works: Encourages systematic thinking, reduces errors on complex problems.

Example

Prompt:

When answering "Should we launch this product in Q2?", generate 3-5 sub-questions that help answer it. Answer each sub-question. Then provide the final recommendation. Product: AI-powered scheduling assistant Context: [product details, market research]

Model output:

Sub-questions: 1. Is the product ready for market (feature completeness, stability)? 2. What is the competitive landscape in Q2? 3. Do we have adequate resources for Q2 launch? 4. What are the market conditions and timing factors? [Answers each sub-question...] Final Recommendation: Yes, launch in Q2 because...

Question refinement pattern

Pattern: Ask the model to suggest a better version of your question before answering.

Template: "Suggest a better version of this question, then answer both versions."

Why it works: Helps identify ambiguities and ensures you're asking the right question.

Example

Your question: "How do I improve customer retention?"

Model's refined version: "What are the top 3 data-driven strategies to improve B2B SaaS customer retention in the first 90 days, with measurable KPIs?"

The refined version is more specific and actionable.

Constraint pattern

Pattern: Define explicit constraints or limitations on the output.

Template: "[Task]. Constraints: [LIST OF CONSTRAINTS]"

Why it works: Prevents unwanted behaviors, enforces requirements.

Examples

  • "Write a product description. Constraints: Under 100 words, no technical jargon, emphasize benefits not features"
  • "Generate SQL query. Constraints: Use only SELECT statements (no INSERT/UPDATE/DELETE), include comments"
  • "Draft email response. Constraints: Professional tone, acknowledge their concern, offer specific next steps"

 Advanced Techniques: Chain of Thought & ReAct

For complex reasoning tasks, advanced prompting techniques can significantly improve accuracy.

Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting

Chain of Thought: Encourage the model to show its reasoning steps before giving a final answer.

Key phrase: "Let's think step by step" or "Show your reasoning"

Why it works: Breaking problems into steps reduces errors on multi-step reasoning tasks.

Example: Math Problem

Without CoT:

Q: A store has 23 apples. They sell 17 and receive a shipment of 45. How many apples do they have? A: 51

With CoT:

Q: A store has 23 apples. They sell 17 and receive a shipment of 45. How many apples do they have? Let's think step by step. A: Step 1: Start with 23 apples Step 2: Sell 17 apples: 23 - 17 = 6 apples remaining Step 3: Receive shipment of 45: 6 + 45 = 51 apples Final answer: 51 apples

The explicit reasoning makes the answer verifiable and builds trust.

Zero-shot CoT

Zero-shot CoT: Simply add "Let's think step by step" to your prompt—no examples needed.

Use for: Quick reasoning improvements without crafting examples.

Example: Business Decision

Should we prioritize feature A (requested by 80% of users) or feature B (requested by 20% of enterprise customers who pay 70% of revenue)? Let's think step by step.

The model will break down revenue impact, strategic value, implementation cost, etc.

Few-shot CoT

Few-shot CoT: Provide examples that include reasoning steps.

Use for: Complex domain-specific reasoning where you want to demonstrate the thinking process.

Example Template

Q: [Example question 1] A: Let's think step by step. [Step 1 reasoning] [Step 2 reasoning] [Step 3 reasoning] Therefore, [answer]. Q: [Example question 2] A: Let's think step by step. [Steps...] Therefore, [answer]. Q: [Your actual question] A:

ReAct (Reasoning + Acting)

ReAct: Combine reasoning with actions. The model alternates between thinking about what to do and taking actions.

Pattern: Thought → Action → Observation → Thought → Action → ...

Use for: Multi-step tasks requiring information gathering or tool use (common in AI agents).

Example: Research Task

Task: Find the current CEO of Microsoft and their previous role. Thought: I need to search for Microsoft's current CEO. Action: Search "Microsoft CEO 2024" Observation: Satya Nadella is the CEO of Microsoft. Thought: Now I need to find Satya Nadella's previous role before becoming CEO. Action: Search "Satya Nadella previous role before CEO" Observation: Nadella was Executive VP of Microsoft's Cloud and Enterprise group. Answer: Satya Nadella is the current CEO of Microsoft. His previous role was Executive VP of Cloud and Enterprise.

 Prompt Engineering Best Practices

Be clear and specific

  • Use precise language—avoid ambiguity
  • Specify exactly what you want (format, length, tone)
  • Break complex tasks into smaller sub-tasks
  • Define technical terms if they have multiple meanings

Iterate and experiment

  • Start simple, add complexity as needed
  • Test prompts on multiple examples to ensure consistency
  • Keep a "prompt library" of successful patterns
  • A/B test different phrasings for critical use cases

Provide sufficient context

  • Include relevant background information
  • Don't assume the model knows your specific business context
  • Be explicit about constraints and requirements
  • Balance context (too much wastes tokens, too little produces poor results)

Use delimiters and structure

  • Separate instructions from content (use triple quotes, XML tags, etc.)
  • Use numbered lists for multi-step instructions
  • Organize complex prompts with headings and sections
  • Example: Summarize the text delimited by triple quotes: """[TEXT HERE]"""

What to avoid

  • ❌ Vague instructions ("make it better", "analyze this")
  • ❌ Assuming the model has current information (it has a knowledge cutoff)
  • ❌ Asking for opinions on subjective matters as if they're facts
  • ❌ Over-complicating when simple prompts work fine
  • ❌ Not validating outputs for critical use cases

Helpful Tip

Prompt engineering is an iterative skill. Don't expect perfect results on your first attempt. Build a library of proven prompts for common tasks, and continuously refine based on what works. Many organizations see 10-50% improvement in LLM outputs just by applying structured prompt patterns.
